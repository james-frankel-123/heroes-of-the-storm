\title{When Similar Accuracy Produces Different Policies:\\ Value Function Features Matter More Than Aggregate Metrics}
\author{}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We show that learned value functions with similar aggregate accuracy can produce substantially different policies when used for sequential decision-making. In a controlled experiment using combinatorial team selection (Heroes of the Storm drafting), three value functions spanning 56.5--57.9\% test accuracy yield policies that differ on over 90\% of decisions. The key variable is whether the value function encodes \textit{compositional} features---information about how selected elements interact, not just their individual quality. A model with compositional features selects balanced teams 17.5 percentage points more often ($\chi^2\!=\!30.1$, $p\!<\!0.0001$) and reduces degenerate compositions from 76\% to 58\% ($\chi^2\!=\!35.5$, $p\!<\!0.0001$), despite only a 1.4pp accuracy advantage. Cross-evaluation reveals the compositional model deliberately sacrifices individual element strength for interaction quality---a trade-off invisible to aggregate accuracy. We connect this to out-of-distribution value overestimation in offline reinforcement learning: models trained on data where most compositions are reasonable cannot penalize unreasonable ones, and greedy policies exploit exactly this gap. Notably, the enriched model still produces 57.7\% degenerate compositions, showing that feature engineering partially mitigates but does not solve the underlying problem.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

When a learned value function guides a search algorithm, a natural assumption is that models with higher held-out accuracy produce better policies. We show that this assumption fails: value functions with nearly identical aggregate accuracy can encode very different inductive biases, and these biases determine whether the resulting policy makes reasonable decisions.

The core insight is that aggregate accuracy conflates two capabilities: estimating the value of states the policy will actually visit, and estimating the value of states it will not. A value function accurate on the training distribution may be arbitrarily wrong on states that a greedy policy discovers through optimization pressure. If those errors are systematically optimistic, the policy seeks out bad states that appear good.

We demonstrate this in Heroes of the Storm (HotS) draft selection, a sequential two-player game where teams alternately ban and pick characters from a pool of 90. The resulting 5v5 team compositions determine roughly 55--58\% of game outcomes. This domain suits our study because the state space requires generalization ($\binom{90}{5}^2 \!\approx\! 10^{13}$ matchups), training data concentrates on reasonable compositions, and the gap between individual element quality and compositional quality is large and measurable.

We construct three win probability models with similar test accuracy (56.5--57.9\%) but different feature sets and show: (1) they disagree on over 90\% of decisions; (2) the enriched model produces significantly better compositions ($p\!<\!0.0001$); (3) cross-evaluation reveals it trades individual element strength for compositional quality; and (4) it still produces 57.7\% degenerate compositions, demonstrating the limits of feature engineering as a mitigation. We frame these findings through out-of-distribution (OOD) value overestimation from offline RL~\cite{kumar2020conservative,levine2020offline}.

\section{Problem Setup}

A HotS draft consists of 16 sequential actions: 6 bans and 10 picks, alternating between two teams. The state $s_t$ comprises multi-hot vectors for each team's picks and bans ($\in \{0,1\}^{90}$ each), map identity (14 maps), skill tier (3 tiers), and draft step---289 dimensions total.

A win probability model $V_\theta(s) \in [0,1]$ estimates $P(\text{team\,0 wins} \mid s)$ for terminal states. We use greedy search: for each candidate hero, complete the draft via Generic Draft (GD) opponent models trained on 275K replays, evaluate the terminal state with $V_\theta$, and select the highest-scoring candidate. All $\sim$84 candidates are evaluated via batched forward passes.

All models train on 275,285 Storm League replays with team-swap augmentation: every sample $(s, y)$ generates $(s', 1\!-\!y)$ by swapping teams and all team-indexed features, doubling the training set to 550K and ensuring symmetric treatment of both sides.

Table~\ref{tab:models} shows the three models. The enriched model adds 86 features: per-team counts of 9 fine-grained roles (18 dims), pairwise hero counter and synergy statistics (76 dims), and empirical composition win rates (4 dims). The composition WR feature maps each team's 5 heroes to a role-tuple category (e.g., Tank--Bruiser--Healer--2$\times$Ranged Assassin) and looks up its empirical win rate from a community replay database covering 9.0M games across three skill tiers (121--164 distinct compositions per tier). Unknown compositions receive a pessimistic default of 33\% WR. The feature encodes $(w\!-\!50)/10$ and $\log(1\!+\!g)/15$ per team, where $w$ is win rate and $g$ is game count.

Despite the enriched model's substantially richer input, the accuracy gap is only 1.4pp.

\section{Results}

We evaluate each model on 480 draft scenarios (stratified by map, tier, and side), producing 1,440 total drafts in 9.7 minutes on 4 GPUs. Each terminal state is cross-evaluated by all three models.

Table~\ref{tab:comp} shows the enriched model selects healers 17.5pp more often than naive ($\chi^2\!=\!30.1$). The hero-strength model performs \textit{worse} than naive on healer selection (39.0\% vs 49.0\%), suggesting individual strength features actively steer the model toward high-WR damage dealers and away from healers, whose individual win rates are moderate. Agreement between naive and enriched is below 10\% at every draft phase despite only 1.4pp accuracy difference. On 21 hand-crafted sanity tests, the enriched model scores 17/21 vs 14/21 (naive) and 12/21 (hero strength), with the largest advantage on ``trap'' scenarios where individually-strong heroes appear in degenerate compositions.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{The Accuracy--Policy Disconnect}

A systematic sweep across 4,100 model configurations ($2^{10}$ subsets of 10 feature groups $\times$ 2 architectures $\times$ 2 learning rates) reveals that the features most important for accuracy differ from those most important for policy quality. Pairwise hero interaction features dominate accuracy (+4.5\% marginal impact), while the composition WR feature contributes only +0.2\% to accuracy. Yet \texttt{comp\_wr} provides the signal that makes the model assign 17.2\% WP to a 5-tank team (vs 44.7\% from the naive model) and is central to the 18pp reduction in degenerate compositions. A feature that barely moves the accuracy metric can fundamentally change which actions the model recommends.

Among the 50 drafts with the largest naive-vs-enriched evaluation gap, 41/50 (82\%) had no healer and 48/50 (96\%) paired individually-strong heroes (high-WR bruisers and melee assassins) with degenerate team structure. The naive model consistently chose these heroes over available healers because it cannot represent the catastrophic interaction between ``no healer'' and ``fifth damage dealer.''

\subsection{The Residual Gap}

The enriched model still produces 57.7\% degenerate compositions. This residual rate is one of our most informative findings: feature engineering partially mitigates but does not solve OOD overestimation. Three factors contribute. First, \textit{search horizon}: greedy evaluation cannot reason about future availability (``will a healer still be available at pick 5?''). Second, \textit{training concentration}: over 95\% of training teams include a healer, so the base model's prior for any composition is ``roughly 50\% WP''---the enriched features correct this but cannot fully override it. Third, \textit{feature granularity}: composition WR captures role balance but not fine-grained hero-specific interactions within a role category. Multi-step lookahead or synthetic OOD penalties may be needed to close this gap.

\section{Connection to Offline RL}

The behavior we observe is a specific instance of OOD value overestimation from offline RL~\cite{kumar2020conservative,levine2020offline}. A value function $V_\theta$ trained on dataset $\mathcal{D}$ may be arbitrarily wrong for states underrepresented in $\mathcal{D}$, and a greedy policy specifically seeks states where $V_\theta$ is overoptimistic. In our setting, $\mathcal{D}$ is 275K replays where 95\%+ of teams have healers; the OOD states are healerless compositions that greedy search constructs by repeatedly selecting individually-strong heroes.

Our enriched features function as pessimistic value correction analogous to CQL~\cite{kumar2020conservative}: the 33\% default WR for unknown compositions is an explicit pessimistic prior, and role-count features let the model distinguish standard from degenerate compositions. This is feature-level pessimism rather than algorithmic pessimism. The residual 57.7\% degenerate rate demonstrates its limits: the correction applies only where features provide signal, and greedy search finds degenerate states the features do not fully cover. DraftRec~\cite{lee2022draftrec} addresses this from the policy side, restricting recommendations to observed hero combinations---implicitly constraining the policy to remain near the training distribution as in BEAR~\cite{kumar2019stabilizing}.

\section{Related Work}

Chen et al.~\cite{chen2018drafting} proposed MCTS-based drafting for Dota~2 using a win predictor as the value function but did not examine how value function features affect policy quality---the gap our work addresses. Houk and Chaslot~\cite{houk2019monte} studied Monte Carlo draft recommendation for HotS. Summerville et al.~\cite{summerville2016draft} modeled professional pick sequences as behavioral cloning. The sensitivity of planning to value function design has been studied in game-playing~\cite{silver2016mastering} and robotics~\cite{kalashnikov2018qtopt}; we provide a controlled example where near-identical accuracy yields 90\%+ decision disagreement.

\section{Conclusion}

Aggregate accuracy is a misleading proxy for value function quality. Models spanning 56.5--57.9\% accuracy produce policies disagreeing on 90\%+ of decisions. The enriched model reduces degenerate compositions by 18pp ($p\!<\!0.0001$) through features that contribute only 0.2\% to accuracy. The residual 57.7\% degenerate rate shows feature engineering is necessary but insufficient: it partially mitigates OOD overestimation without solving it. We advocate for policy-level evaluation of value functions and note that stronger interventions---multi-step search, policy constraints, or synthetic OOD penalties---are likely needed to fully address the problem.

\textit{Limitations.} Single game domain, greedy (depth-1) search, fixed opponent model. The specific gap may differ under deeper search or in other combinatorial domains.
